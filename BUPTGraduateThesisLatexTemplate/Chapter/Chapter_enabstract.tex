\begin{enabstract}
With the rapid development of low Earth orbit satellite communication systems, the fast detection and accurate localization of illegal LEO satellite terminals in complex urban environments has become an important problem in radio security supervision. Compared with conventional fixed monitoring stations, low-cost UAV platforms provide higher mobility, more flexible deployment, and adjustable observation perspectives, and therefore offer a new technical approach for terminal investigation in dense urban areas. However, under the combined influence of urban multipath, non-line-of-sight propagation, local blockage, and the limitations of platform payload and onboard computing capability, traditional geometric localization methods and static fusion strategies often fail to achieve a satisfactory balance among localization accuracy, environmental adaptability, and engineering practicability. To address these issues, this thesis studies the localization and identification of illegal LEO satellite terminals based on a low-cost UAV platform from two aspects: single-target continuous localization and multi-target concurrent localization.

For the single-target continuous localization problem, where the quality of heterogeneous observations varies significantly in complex environments and fixed-weight fusion is difficult to maintain, a physics-aware deep reinforcement learning based adaptive fusion localization method is proposed. First, a weighted fusion model of heterogeneous localization information is established, and the dynamic weight adjustment problem is formulated as a Markov decision process. Then, based on ray-tracing scene modeling, physical prior features including RMS delay spread, RSSI variance, and geometric consistency are extracted to construct a physics-aware state space. On this basis, a Physics-Aware Deep Q-Network, namely PA-DQN, is designed. By introducing an incremental action space and a reward function that jointly considers short-term accuracy and long-term smoothness, the proposed method realizes dynamic and adaptive adjustment of fusion weights for multi-source observations. Experimental results show that, compared with single-source methods, traditional filtering methods, and baseline reinforcement learning methods, the proposed approach achieves better overall localization accuracy, tail-error control, trajectory smoothness, and robustness under low signal-to-noise ratio conditions.

For the multi-target concurrent localization problem, where sparse spatial scanning, local multi-peak responses, and multipath ghosts make traditional peak-based decision methods unreliable, a UAV signal imaging and robust cooperative inference based localization method is further proposed. First, a propagation field model for complex urban environments is constructed based on ray tracing, and high-fidelity spatial response simulation is implemented with OSM-based scene modeling and a MATLAB toolchain. Then, through RSSI grid mapping and Radio Map reconstruction, discrete spatial observations are transformed into radio heat maps with regional structure, which enables the cross-modal conversion from the numerical domain to the image domain. Furthermore, considering the random node dropout that may occur in multi-UAV cooperative networks, a teacher-student knowledge distillation framework is designed. The teacher model trained with complete observations is used to supervise the student model under incomplete observations, so that the student network can still approximate the decision capability of the complete network in the presence of missing nodes. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method effectively alleviates performance degradation caused by random node dropout and maintains relatively stable localization performance under medium and high dropout ratios, which confirms the effectiveness of knowledge distillation for robust cooperative localization in complex environments.

In summary, this thesis establishes a research framework for the localization and identification of illegal LEO satellite terminals based on a low-cost UAV platform, covering system modeling, propagation mechanism analysis, single-target adaptive fusion localization, multi-target concurrent localization, and dropout-robust cooperative inference. The results indicate that the combination of physical priors, deep reinforcement learning, signal imaging, and knowledge distillation can effectively improve the accuracy, stability, and engineering applicability of illegal LEO satellite terminal localization in complex urban environments. The work of this thesis can provide useful references for wireless sensing and illegal terminal inspection based on low-cost UAV platforms in complex scenarios.

\end{enabstract}
